# **B. Formal Lemma for the Informant–Handler Game**  
*(ready for LaTeX insertion)*

```latex
\begin{lemma}[Collapse of Exclusivity in Multi-Actor Intelligence Environments]
\label{lemma:exclusivity}
Consider an environment in which a problem $P$ is observable by a set of
actors $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, A_2, \dots, A_n\}$, each with independent
observation channels and response capabilities. Let $S$ be a source
interacting strategically with a handler $H$ in the Informant--Handler
Game.

If at least one additional actor $A_k \neq H$ has a non-zero probability
of observing $P$ or generating intelligence about $P$, then the
assumption that $H$ is the sole consumer of $S$'s information collapses.
Formally, if
\[
\Pr(A_k \text{ observes } P) > 0,
\]
then the expected payoff to $S$ from fabricating exclusive intelligence
satisfies
\[
\mathbb{E}[U_S(F \mid \text{exclusivity})]
>
\mathbb{E}[U_S(F \mid \text{multi-actor})],
\]
because the probability of contradiction, cross-verification, or
disconfirmation increases with the number of independent actors.

Consequently, in any multi-actor environment, the equilibrium set of
strategies for $S$ shifts away from fabrications that rely on exclusivity
assumptions, and toward either (i) truthful reporting, (ii) low-stakes
distortion, or (iii) fabrications that do not depend on uniqueness of
access.
\end{lemma}
```

# **Interpretation (for your paper’s prose section)**

This lemma formalizes the intuition behind your GitHub text:

- When **multiple actors** can observe or respond to the same problem,  
- **no informant** can safely assume their story will be the only one in circulation,  
- and therefore **fabrications that rely on exclusivity become unstable**.

This is a direct extension of your Informant–Handler Game:

- It adds a *meta‑layer* where the handler is not the only consumer of signals.  
- It introduces **cross‑verification pressure** from other actors.  
- It shows how multi‑actor environments **raise the cost of lying**.
